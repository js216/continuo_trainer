- Boredom: Does this task feel repetitive or under-stimulating?
- Skill mismatch: Is the difficulty exceeding my current skill?
- Undefined next step: Is the next concrete physical action unclear?
- Value misalignment: Does this task feel disconnected from what I care
  about?
- Resentment: Does this feel coerced or imposed against my will?
- Fear of judgment: Am I anxious about being judged (by self or others)
  for the result?
- Shame from past failure: Does this task carry shame from prior
  failures in this domain?
- Cognitive load: Does this demand heavy mental effort with no autopilot
  to lean on?
- Low self-efficacy: Do I doubt my effort will actually pay off?
- Tempting alternatives: Are more attractive alternatives easily within
  reach right now?
- Interruptions: Is my environment full of distractions or interruption
  sources?
- Delayed reward: Is the payoff far away in time?
- No progress signal: Is in-task feedback or visible progress absent?
- No accountability: Am I working alone with no one expecting a result
  from me?

### Pre-run

1. Hot thought (one sentence). The specific belief driving avoidance.
Phrased as a prediction. Examples: "If I sit down for 25 min, I'll get
stuck and produce nothing usable" / "My effort on this won't measurably
move the task forward."

2. Belief rating, pre (0–100). How much you believe the hot thought
right now, gut number. Write it down. This is measurement #1.

3. Operational outcome. What observable thing in the next 25 min would
disconfirm the thought? Make it concrete and binary. Examples: "I
produce ≥1 line of code that compiles" / "I write ≥3 sentences I'd keep
tomorrow." This is measurement #2 (outcome data).

4. Distress rating, pre (0–100). How aversive does starting feel right
now?  Measurement #3.

### Run (25 min, single block)

- Phone in another room. Single window. Timer on for 25 min.
- Work on the task. Don't evaluate, don't edit, don't judge. Just
  produce.
- When the timer rings, stop even if mid-flow. Stopping on time is part
  of the protocol — it prevents the experiment becoming an endurance
  test that you'll avoid repeating.

### Debrief (5 min, immediately after)

5. Outcome (binary). Did the observable from line 3 happen? Yes/no. Plus
one sentence of what actually got produced.
6. Belief rating, post (0–100). Re-rate the hot thought knowing what
just happened.
7. Distress rating, post (0–100). Re-rate aversiveness now that you've
done it.
8. Revised thought (one sentence). Given the data, what's a more
accurate version of the prediction? Not a pep talk — a calibrated
restatement. Example: "When I sit down for 25 min, I usually produce 1–2
usable units, less than I'd like but more than zero."

Did it work?

The intervention is working if any one of these is true:
- Belief drop ≥20 points (pre vs. post on line 2 → 6). Beck's clinical
  threshold for a meaningful single-session shift.
- Distress drop ≥15 points (line 4 → 7). Indicates anticipatory > actual
  aversiveness, which is the avoidance loop weakening.
- Outcome = yes (line 5). Direct disconfirmation, regardless of how the
  ratings move.

If none move, the hot thought on line 1 was wrong — it isn't what's
actually blocking you. Re-run with a different thought (try "this task
is boring and I'd rather do X" or "I resent having to do this").

Why this one

Your factor profile shows the avoidance is predictive ("won't pay off,"
"I'll fail again"), not valuative (you care, -2). Predictions are
testable in a single session; values aren't. One run gives you data;
three runs over a week gives you a trend line that beats any amount of
self-talk.
